A problem for reason-based actions.

People can misattribute reasons to their actions after the fact. Ada might say she acted to protect her cat, but unconscious motives like frustration may have really driven her. This challenges how we distinguish genuine reasons from post hoc rationalizations — especially when unconscious processes play a role.

[[Railton (2017)]] distinguishes genuine reasons from rationalizations by examining whether the agent’s internal evaluative system (i.e. [[affective system]]) actually guided the behavior. If unconscious affective modeling drove the action, then the actor did act for a reason — even if they misreport it later. But if the explanation is disconnected from the causal model, it's merely a rationalization.
