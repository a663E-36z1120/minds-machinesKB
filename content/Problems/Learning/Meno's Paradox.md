**Meno’s paradox** (also known as **the Meno problem**) originates from Plato’s dialogue *[[Meno]]*, where the character Meno poses a challenge to [[What Does It Mean to Learn?]], specifically in the context of discovery, where we learn truly novel concepts external to us:

> *"How will you look for something when you don't know what it is? If you find it, how will you know it's what you were looking for?"*

 **In summary, the problem is:**
- If you **already know** something, there's no need to learn it.
- If you **don’t know** it at all, you wouldn’t even recognize it if you found it.
- So, **how is learning possible**?

**[[Plato]]**'s response is the theory of **[[anamnesis]]**.

A modern response by [[Clark Glymour]] and [[Kevin Kelly]] in  [[Glymour & Kelly (1992)]] is that learning must proceed **without certainty**, often by **eliminating false hypotheses** over time rather than recognizing truth directly.
