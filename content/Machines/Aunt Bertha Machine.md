Proposed in [[Block (1981)]], the **“Aunt Bertha machine”** is Ned Block’s tongue‑in‑cheek name for a hypothetical, utterly mindless device that can nevertheless breeze through a [[Turing Test]]. In short, the Aunt Bertha machine is a lookup‑table chatbot that exposes the gap between intelligent behaviour and intelligent *information‑processing*.

  - A team of programmers first enumerates every *finite* “sensible” conversation that could occur within, say, an hour.  
  - They store those dialogue strings (or, more efficiently, branches of a dialogue tree) on tape.  
  - During the test the machine receives the interrogator’s first sentence *A*, searches for all stored strings that begin with *A*, picks one at random and outputs the second sentence *B*; on the next turn it looks for strings that begin *A B C*, and so on. 
  - The programmers often imagine the replies as ones “Aunt Bertha” would give if her mischievous nephew plunked her in front of a teletype and asked her to answer silly questions, hence the nickname. 

Block concedes that realistically Aunt Bertha Machine is practically impossible due to [[combinatorial explosion]], where by some estimates an hour-long Turing test would require more entries in the lookup-table than the number of particles in the universe.
