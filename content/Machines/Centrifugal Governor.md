A centrifugal governor is a mechanical [[control system]] that controls the speed of an engine by regulating the flow of fuel or working fluid, so as to maintain a near-constant speed.

**How it works:**
1. **Centrifugal Force:** The governor's rotating weights, often called flyballs, are connected to the engine's rotating shaft. As the engine speeds up, the flyballs are thrown outward due to **centrifugal force**.
2. **Control:** This outward movement of the flyballs is connected to a linkage that modulates the flow of fuel or working fluid (like steam) to the engine (usually via some sort of valve or similar mechanism).
3. **Feedback:** 
	- If the engine speeds up, the flyballs move out, reducing the fuel or working fluid, slowing the engine down. 
	- If the engine slows down, the flyballs move in, increasing the fuel or working fluid, speeding the engine up
