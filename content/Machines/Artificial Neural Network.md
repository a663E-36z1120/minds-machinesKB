**Artificial neural networks (ANNs)** are computational models of how [[Biological Neuron]]s perform computation. ANNs are made of simple processing units (“neurons”) connected by weighted links. Each unit sums its inputs, applies a non‑linear activation, and passes the result forward. By adjusting the connection weights through a learning rule (e.g. [[Backpropagation]]), the network can approximate any functions given the correct configurations (see [[Universal Approximation Theorem]]).

**A Brief History**
- **1943**: In ***"A Logical Calculus of the Ideas Immanent to Nervous Activity"***, [[Warren McCulloch]] and [[Walter Pitts]] proposed a mathematical model of the nervous system as a network of simple logical elements, and demonstrated that their model could perform all logical functions. (see [[Universal Approximation Theorem]])
- **1949**: [[Donald Hebb]] proposed the _Hebbian_ learning principle: synapses strengthen when pre‑ and postsynaptic neurons fire together (“cells that fire together wire together”), linking biological and artificial learning.
- **1986**: [[David Rumelhart]] and [[James McClelland]] proposed in their body of works ***"Parallel Distributed Processing”***, the core ideas of [[Connectionism]] as a computational model to understand cognition, taking ANNs beyond perceptrons multi-layer networks.
- **2006**: [[Geoffrey Hinton]] and colleagues lays the groundwork for [[Deep Learning]] with [[deep belief netork]]s.
- **2015**: [[Yann LeCun]], [[Yoshua Bengio]], and Geoffrey Hinton publishes the paper ***"Deep Learning"***.
