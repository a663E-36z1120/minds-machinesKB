The main computational unit of the animal and [[human brain]].

A biological neuron converts graded chemical inputs into a digital‑like electrical spike, routes that spike rapidly down its axon, and converts it back into graded chemical signals—all while continuously adjusting connection strengths to encode experience:

1. **Resting state (‑70 mV)**  
    • A sodium–potassium pump plus leak channels keep the inside of the cell negative by exporting 3 Na⁺ and importing 2 K⁺ each ATP cycle.  
    • This electrochemical gradient is the neuron’s “charged battery.”
2. **Input reception (dendrites & soma)**  
    • Thousands of synapses release neurotransmitters that bind to ligand‑gated ion channels or metabotropic receptors.  
    • Each synaptic event produces a tiny voltage change — excitatory postsynaptic potential (EPSP, depolarising) or inhibitory postsynaptic potential (IPSP, hyperpolarising).  
    • EPSPs and IPSPs spread passively and sum both spatially and temporally across the dendritic tree.
3. **Integration & decision (axon hillock / initial segment)**  
    • When the net membrane potential at the hillock crosses a threshold (≈ ‑55 mV), voltage‑gated Na⁺ channels snap open.
4. **Action potential (all‑or‑none spike)**  
    a. **Depolarisation (≤ +30 mV)** – Na⁺ rushes in.  
    b. **Repolarisation** – Na⁺ channels inactivate; voltage‑gated K⁺ channels open, letting K⁺ out.  
    c. **Hyperpolarisation (undershoot)** – excess K⁺ outflow makes the cell briefly more negative than rest.  
    d. **Refractory period** – inactivated Na⁺ channels prevent another spike for ~1–2 ms, enforcing one‑way propagation and limiting firing rate.
5. **Propagation (axon)**  
    • The spike regenerates at successive nodes of Ranvier. Myelin sheaths between nodes speed conduction via saltatory (jumping) transmission.
6. **Output transmission (axon terminal)**  
    • The arriving spike opens voltage‑gated Ca²⁺ channels; Ca²⁺ influx triggers synaptic vesicles to fuse with the presynaptic membrane.  
    • Vesicles release neurotransmitter molecules into the synaptic cleft within ~1 ms.
7. **Postsynaptic effect**  
    • Neurotransmitters diffuse (≈ 20 nm) and bind to receptors on the next cell, starting the cycle anew.  
    • Signal termination occurs via reuptake pumps, enzymatic breakdown, or diffusion.
8. **Plasticity (learning mechanisms)**  
    • Activity‑dependent changes—e.g., long‑term potentiation/depression (LTP/LTD), spine growth, altered receptor density—modify synaptic strength, enabling learning and memory.
