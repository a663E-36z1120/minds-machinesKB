[[Cameron Buckner]]’s paper, *“Empiricism without magic: transformational abstraction in deep convolutional neural networks,”* proposes a novel, empiricist account of abstraction that avoids positing mysterious innate faculties. The paper bridges classic philosophical issues about how abstract concepts arise from sensory experience with the modern successes of Deep [[Convolutional Neural Network]] (DCNNs) in tasks such as object recognition and image classification.

The paper revisists historical problems with empiricism such as [[Locke's Triangles]] for how the mind form coherent abstract representations (e.g., of a “triangle”) when sensory inputs are always particular and variable. For these, Buckner proposes the idea of ***[[Transformational Abstraction]]*** as a solution.

Specifically, Buckner argues that the success of DCNNs supports an empiricist account of abstraction — abstract representations can emerge entirely through experience and learning, with no need for pre-specified, innate structures. The paper further suggests that similar transformational processes might underlie human perceptual and cognitive systems, as evidenced by the hierarchical organization observed in the mammalian visual cortex.
