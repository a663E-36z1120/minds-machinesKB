[[John Locke]]’s discussion of the “triangles” is a classic example used to illustrate a central problem in [[empiricism]] and its view on abstraction:

- **Abstract vs. Particulars:**  
    Locke argued that all our ideas start from particular, concrete experiences. When it comes to something like a triangle, we encounter countless examples—each with different sizes, angles, orientations, and other variations. Yet, despite these differences, we form a single abstract concept of “triangle.”
    
- **The Problem of Inconsistency:**  
    Locke highlighted that the abstract idea of a triangle must somehow capture the essential quality ("triangularity") while simultaneously leaving out the many idiosyncratic details (such as exact angle measures or side lengths). This raises the question: How can the mind form a coherent, general idea from a collection of inconsistent particular instances?
    
According to Locke, the abstract idea of a triangle isn’t directly given in sensory experience but is constructed by the mind. It “subtracts” the irrelevant details from each specific exemplar to arrive at an idea that represents the common, defining features of all triangles. Yet, this process seems mysterious because the resulting abstract concept appears to contain “all and none” of the particulars’ variations.
