[[Peter Railton]] in *'At the Core of Our Capacity to Act for a  Reason: The Affective System and Evaluative Model-Based Learning and Control'* (2017) makes the distinction between two kinds of reasons:  
1. **Causal-explanatory Reasons** explain behavior by identifying causes — factors that played a role in producing an action, regardless of the actor's awareness or intention.
2. **Putative-rationale Reasons** explain behavior by identifying the _reasons the agent took themselves to have_—factors they saw as justifying or favoring the action. Crucially, a putative rationale must also be causally effective (not just an after-the-fact rationalization) to count as a true reason _for which_ someone acted.

Railton outlines 4 problems of what it means to act _for a reason_:
1. [[Deviant Causal Chain Problem]]
2. [[Agency-without-Regress Problem]]
3. [[Nondeliberative Attunement Problem]]
4. [[Rationalization Problem]]

To these four problems, Railton proposes a unified solution that the [[affective system]], through evaluative model-based learning and control. More specifically, the affective system in animals have several hallmarks of a [[control system]], where it:
- Represents value.
- Guides action (as a [[Model]] that can operate independently of immediate environmental stimulus and rewards).
- Connects imagination (temporal feed forward), perception, and memory (temporal feed-back).
- Is capable of adaptive and complex computation.

In unison, these form what we call "[[model-based]] affective control".
