[[Peter Railton]] in *'At the Core of Our Capacity to Act for a  Reason: The Affective System and Evaluative Model-Based Learning and Control'* (2017) makes the distinction between two kinds of reasons:  
1. **Causal-explanatory Reasons** explain behavior by identifying causes — factors that played a role in producing an action, regardless of the actor's awareness or intention.
2. **Putative-rationale Reasons** explain behavior by identifying the _reasons the agent took themselves to have_—factors they saw as justifying or favoring the action. Crucially, a putative rationale must also be causally effective (not just an after-the-fact rationalization) to count as a true reason _for which_ someone acted.
