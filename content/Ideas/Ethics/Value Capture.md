[[Thi Nguyen]] defines ***value capture*** in the paper with the same name (see [[Nguyen (2024)]]) as the process by which an external value specification – typically offered by institutions through clear, standardized metrics – is internalized so completely that it governs one’s practical reasoning. 

Rather than engaging in a nuanced, reflective process to refine one’s own values, the individual or group adopts these prefab, externally derived values as if they were self-generated.

Nguyen outlines the following mechanism for how value capture takes place:
- **Outsourcing Value Deliberation:**  
    When people buy into an institutional metric (like a Fitbit’s step count, law school rankings, or GPA), they are effectively outsourcing the complex task of value deliberation. The external metric provides clear, immediate feedback and seemingly objective criteria for success, but at the cost of sidelining more subtle, context-specific aspects of what one truly values.
    
- **Standardization and Quantification:**  
    Institutions favor metrics because they are designed to be standardized and quantifiable. Such metrics are optimized for mass communication and aggregation—ensuring that they are easily transmitted and understood across different organizational levels. However, this standardization also means that they resist personalization, thus narrowing the range of values individuals can adopt.
    
- **Cognitive Fluency and Its Seductiveness:**  
    Nguyen discusses how the very clarity and ease of processing (cognitive fluency) that characterize quantified metrics make them particularly seductive. The familiarity and apparent precision of numbers engender trust and can lead individuals to overvalue these metrics relative to more ambiguous, qualitative values.
