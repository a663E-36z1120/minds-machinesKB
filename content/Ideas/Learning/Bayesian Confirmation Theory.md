**Bayesian confirmation theory** is a framework for understanding how evidence affects the credibility of hypotheses using the tools of **Bayesian probability** as developed by [[Thomas Bayes]] (under the broader umbrella of Bayesian [[epistemology]]).

Bayesian confirmation theory models how **beliefs should be updated** when new evidence is obtained, following **[[Bayes’ Theorem]]**:
$$P(H|E) = \frac{P(E|H) \cdot P(H)}{P(E)}$$
Where:
- $P(H)$ is the **prior probability** of hypothesis $H$,
- $P(E∣H)$ is the **likelihood**: the probability of seeing evidence $E$ assuming $H$ is true,
- $P(H|E)$ is the **posterior probability**: how confident we should be in $H$ after observing $E$.
    
In this theory, **evidence $E$ confirms hypothesis $H$** if it raises its probability:
$$P(H|E) > P(H)$$
This gives a **graded**, probabilistic notion of confirmation rather than a binary (proven/disproven) one.
