**The Chisholm–Geach objection** to [[behaviorism]] (sometimes called the _“belief‑condition”_ or _“perfect actor”_ objection) is a classic argument showing why pure behaviorist analyses of mental states cannot work.

[[Roderick Chisholm]] and [[Peter Geach]] pointed out in the 1950s that which behavioural dispositions a person exhibits depends crucially on other mental states he holds. Therefore one cannot define a mental state just by listing stimulus‑response patterns, because the same external situation can elicit very different behaviour depending on the agent’s concurrent beliefs and desires.

**Example**
Take the behaviourist attempt to analyse the state “wanting an ice‑cream cone.”

Behaviourists would say: the person is disposed to reach for and eat an ice‑cream cone when one is presented.

Chisholm and Geach reply: the person will do that only if he believes it is really ice‑cream (not axle‑grease offered as a joke) and only if he doesn’t hold stronger conflicting desires (e.g., to avoid sugar).

Because those auxiliary beliefs and desires are themselves mental states, the analysis is circular—it smuggles in mentality to define mentality.
