In *'[[psychologism]] and [[behaviorism]]'*, [[Ned Block]] defends psychologism and rejects behaviourist attempts to analyse intelligence purely in terms of actual or potential behavior.

Block first reconstructs a “natural” behaviourist position inspired by the [[Turing test]], where [[intelligence]] is the ***disposition*** to give sensible verbal responses to any verbal input; upon this, Block further constructs an improved (neo‑Turing) behaviorist position where intelligence is instead the ***capacity*** to do so. 

Because this capacity formulation appears immune to the classic [[Chisholm–Geach Objection]] and [[“Brain‑in‑a‑vat” Objection]], Block treats it as the strongest remaining behaviourist analysis of intelligence.

Block then constructs a logically possible machine, which he names the "[[Aunt Bertha machine]]" that passes any finite Turing Test without containing a shred of intelligence. Programmers pre‑record every finite “sensible” conversation as strings (or as branches of a dialogue tree). During the test the machine merely _looks up_ the interrogator’s prefix and prints the next pre‑stored line. Internally it performs only trivial searches; all genuine cognition resided in the programmers who prepared the tapes. Behaviourally the Aunt Bertha machine has exactly the capacity the neo‑Turing account requires. Therefore that capacity is **not sufficient** for intelligence, and behaviourism fails.

Block concedes that a brute‑force Aunt Bertha machine would eventually hit practical limits due to [[combinatorial explosion]], but that doesn’t rescue behaviorism. Either:
- the machine is still _logically_ possible, so the behaviourist analysis is conceptually false, or...
- one tightens the definition by adding internal‑processing constraints—thereby abandoning behaviourism and sliding into psychologism.
