**Semantic externalism** is the view in the philosophy of language that a word’s meaning — and the content of our thoughts — is not determined solely by what goes on inside our minds (our beliefs, sensations, or internal representations) but also by external factors. 

Key positions of semantic externalism are:

- **Dependence on External Factors:**  
  Semantic externalism maintains that aspects of meaning arise from the world beyond the individual. This includes the history of a word’s usage, the context in which it is used, and the physical environment or natural conditions in which its referent exists.

- **Causal-Historical Chains:**  
  A central idea is that words acquire their reference through a causal history. For example, when a community first uses a term to designate an object (a process sometimes illustrated by a “baptism” event), that original connection can persist even if individual speakers have limited or mistaken knowledge about the referent.

- **The Role of the [[Linguistic Community]]:**  
  Semantic externalism emphasizes that meaning is often a social achievement. The norms, practices, and expert consensus within a community contribute significantly to how words refer to objects or concepts. A speaker might use a term correctly simply by being part of a linguistic community that has established that term’s meaning over time.

Classic examples include [[Hilary Putnam]]’s [[Twin Earth Scenario]].
