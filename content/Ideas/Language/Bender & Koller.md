*“Climbing towards NLU: On Meaning, Form, and Understanding in the Age of Data”* examines the claim that large neural language models (LMs) truly “understand” language. 

The paper argues that although these models — such as BERT and GPT-2 — excel at numerous natural language processing (NLP) tasks, their impressive performance often leads to overhyped assertions that they capture meaning or comprehension comparable to human-level natural language understanding (NLU).

Bender and Koller makes a distinction between **Language as Form Versus Meaning:**  
    The authors stress that language has two aspects. On one hand, there is the observable form — words, sentences, and patterns of text. On the other hand, there is meaning, which they define as the relation between linguistic form and communicative intent (i.e., the purpose or message behind the utterance). They argue that systems trained solely on form (through prediction tasks) have no inherent way to learn or capture meaning. Meaning, according to Bender and Koller, is not an intrinsic property of textual patterns but emerges when a form is used to express a communicative intent that is grounded in the external world. This external grounding is what allows language to refer to real-world entities and convey information beyond mere statistical patterns.

To illustrate these distinctions, Bender and Koller proposed the [[The Octopus Test]], and argue that the octopus in the test that learned only how to manipulate symbols and forms in the English language fundamentally cannot understand what it is communicating, as it lacks communicative intent and grounding.
