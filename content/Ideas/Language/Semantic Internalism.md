**Semantic internalism** is a view in the philosophy of language and mind that holds that the meaning of words and the content of our thoughts are determined entirely by factors internal to the individual. Key positions are:

- **Mind-Dependent Meaning:**  
    Under semantic internalism, the meanings of words and the contents of thoughts are anchored in the individual's internal mental states — such as beliefs, sensations, cognitive representations, and neural configurations. What one thinks or believes plays the central role in determining meaning.
    
- **Insensitivity to External Circumstances:**  
    According to this view, external factors — such as the physical environment or the historical usage of words by a community — do not fundamentally contribute to an individual's semantic content. Even if the external circumstances changed, as long as the internal mental states remain the same, the meaning remains stable.
    
- **Subject-Centered Understanding:**  
    Semantic internalism suggests that an individual’s conceptual framework is entirely self-contained. In other words, if two people share identical internal cognitive states, they will have the same semantic content, regardless of potential differences in their external surroundings.
    
In contrast to [[semantic externalism]], which argues that meaning is partly determined by an individual’s interaction with the world (for example, through causal-historical chains or community norms), semantic internalism maintains that all necessary criteria for meaning are provided by the mind’s own structure and processes.
