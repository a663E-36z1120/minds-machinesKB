In *“Do Language Models’ Words Refer?”*, Mandelkern & Linzen investigates whether the outputs of language models (LM) can be said to refer to real-world entities — that is, whether the words they produce establish genuine “word-to-world” connections — even though these models are trained solely on text data and lack direct interaction with the external world, or, are they merely “stochastic parrots” — systems that recycle statistical patterns without any true connection to meaning or referents.

The authors' arguments favor the former over the latter through [[Semantic Externalism]], which have the following key implications for language models:

- **Inputs Carry a Natural History:** Although LMs are trained solely on textual inputs, these texts have been produced by human communities over time. Thus, the inputs come with a “natural history” that connects words to the world. This historical chain is not erased when an LM is trained on these texts; in effect, the LM inherits the referential grounding embedded in its training data.
    
- **Community Membership:** A critical question is whether LMs can be considered part of the [[linguistic community]] due to the [[Grounding Problem]]. While LMs lack sensory experiences and possibly many of the cognitive faculties of human speakers, they operate within the linguistic patterns established by that community. The argument goes that even if LMs are not full linguistic agents in the human sense, their outputs might still refer because they are generated by a system whose training data are causally linked to real-world referents.
    
- **Inference vs. World Interaction:** The paper notes that while LMs excel at “word-to-word” connections (inferences based on statistical regularities), this strength may indirectly support referentiality. The referential quality of words does not necessarily require direct world interaction—it can be mediated by the historical and communal background of language, which LMs implicitly learn.

Overall, the paper challenges the view that language models are mere imitators of human language without referential capability. By drawing on externalist theories from philosophy, the authors argue that the training data of LMs, imbued with a rich natural history and communal usage, suffices to ground reference in their outputs.
